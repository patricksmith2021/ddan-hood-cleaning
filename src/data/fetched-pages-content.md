### Section: What Is NFPA 96? (H2)

NFPA 96 is the National Fire Protection Association's standard for ventilation control and fire protection in commercial cooking operations. It outlines how kitchen hoods, ducts, fans, and suppression systems should be installed, maintained, and cleaned to reduce the risk of grease fires.

For restaurants, food trucks, hotels, schools, and other commercial kitchens in Tennessee, NFPA 96 is the fire safety standard used by fire marshals, insurance providers, and health inspectors. At DDAN, we follow this code on every cleaning, repair, or installation job we perform.

### Section: What NFPA 96 Covers (H4) — 6-card grid

This standard outlines best practices for nearly every component of your kitchen exhaust system:

**1. Exhaust Hoods**
- Must be sized and mounted correctly for the cooking equipment underneath
- Built of steel or other approved materials, with grease-tight seams

**2. Ductwork**
- Must be welded, liquid-tight, and sloped toward grease drains
- Requires access panels every 12 feet and at direction changes
- Needs safe clearance from combustible surfaces

**3. Exhaust Fans**
- Should match your cooking load and vent volume
- Must have hinge kits for rooftop cleaning access
- Requires weatherproofing and grease containment

**4. Grease Containment**
- Rooftop containment systems must capture discharged grease before it damages roof surfaces
- Regular maintenance is necessary to prevent clogs, overflows, and environmental hazards

**5. Fire Suppression Systems**
- Must automatically shut off fuel/electricity and discharge wet chemical extinguishants
- Nozzles should be properly placed over appliances and integrated with the hood system
- Systems must be UL 300 rated and regularly maintained

**6. Cleaning & Maintenance**
- Entire system must be cleaned to bare metal
- Frequency depends on volume:
  - Monthly: Solid fuel cooking
  - Quarterly: High-volume kitchens
  - Semiannually: Moderate use
  - Annually: Low-volume or seasonal operations

### Section: What Tennessee Commercial Kitchens Need to Know (H4)

There's no state-issued "NFPA 96 certificate" in Tennessee—but compliance is still expected. Here's how it applies:

**Local Enforcement**
- Fire Marshals perform inspections and will cite violations if your system doesn't meet NFPA 96 standards
- Health Departments also consider ventilation and grease control in their assessments

**Insurance Requirements**
- Most commercial kitchen policies require NFPA 96 maintenance to stay valid
- Claims for fire damage may be denied if your system hasn't been properly cleaned or maintained

**It's About Safety and Liability**
Following NFPA 96 isn't just about passing inspection. It reduces the risk of:
- Grease fires that endanger staff and property
- Shutdowns or costly citations
- Voided insurance coverage

### Section: Understanding Your Restaurant Exhaust Hood System, From Hood to Rooftop Fan (H2)

#### The Core Function: What a Commercial Kitchen Ventilation System Does (H3)

A commercial kitchen ventilation system is designed to efficiently pull grease, smoke, heat, and airborne contaminants away from your cooking area. Its primary job is to safely expel these elements outside your building, maintaining a clean, safe, and compliant environment. This is achieved through a coordinated network of components—each vital for performance, fire safety, and regulatory adherence.

Whether you operate a bustling food truck or a large full-service restaurant, your system is built to meet NFPA 96 standards. Regular maintenance is crucial to ensure everything runs safely and efficiently, protecting your investment and your people.

### Section: Core Components of Your Restaurant Exhaust System (H2)

A typical commercial kitchen ventilation system includes these essential parts:

- Exhaust Hood: Positioned directly above your cooking equipment, the hood is the first point of capture for smoke, steam, and grease-laden vapors.
- Grease Filters (Baffles): Located within the hood, these filters trap grease particles before they can enter and accumulate in the duct system. They are commonly available in stainless steel or aluminum.
- Grease Ductwork: This robust, fire-rated, and grease-tight network of steel channels carries the captured air from the hood up to the rooftop exhaust fan.
- Access Panels: These are strategically placed openings within the ductwork, specifically designed to provide essential access for thorough cleaning and inspection of the duct interior. NFPA 96 requires them every 12 feet and at every change in direction.
- Rooftop Exhaust Fan: This powerful fan actively pulls contaminated air out of the building and safely discharges it into the atmosphere. It must include hinge kits to allow the fan to be tilted open for proper and safe cleaning access.
- Grease Containment System: Installed at the exhaust fan's discharge point, this system catches any expelled grease, preventing it from damaging your roof membrane and the surrounding environment.
- Makeup Air Unit (MUA): This crucial component actively brings in fresh, tempered outdoor air to replace the air that's exhausted by the fan. It balances the air pressure within the kitchen, preventing negative pressure issues like slamming doors or odors escaping into dining areas.
- Fire Suppression System: Integrated directly into the hood system, this automatic system is designed to detect and extinguish flames automatically in the event of a cooking-related fire, protecting your property and staff.

### Section: Airflow Path: How Contaminated Air Moves Through Your System (H2)

Understanding the journey of air through your system clarifies why each component is so important:

1. Cooking Activity: Smoke, steam, and grease-laden vapors are produced from your cookline.
2. Hood Capture: The exhaust hood immediately draws in this contaminated air.
3. Grease Filtration: Grease filters trap a significant portion of the grease particles, preventing them from entering the ductwork.
4. Ductwork Transport: The now-filtered air travels safely through the dedicated steel ductwork, ascending towards the roof.
5. Exhaust Fan Expulsion: The powerful rooftop exhaust fan pulls the air from the ductwork and expels it out of the building.
6. Makeup Air Balance: Simultaneously, the Makeup Air Unit introduces fresh, conditioned air back into the kitchen, maintaining proper air pressure and comfort.

### Section: The Critical Role of Each System Component (H2)

Every single part of your commercial kitchen ventilation system has a vital job. A failure or deficiency in just one area can compromise the entire system's performance, safety, and compliance:

- Dirty Filters = Fire Risk & Poor Capture: When grease filters are overloaded, grease bypasses them, building up in the ducts and significantly increasing fire risk. Capture efficiency drops drastically.
- Leaky Ducts = Hidden Fire Hazards: Grease can escape through unsealed or damaged ductwork, accumulating within walls or ceiling spaces, creating hidden and extremely dangerous fire hazards.
- No Hinge Kit on Fan = Incomplete Cleaning & Fan Damage: Without a hinge kit, rooftop fans cannot be properly cleaned. Grease buildup on fan blades leads to imbalance, overheating, motor failure, and reduced efficiency.
- No Access Panels = Non-Compliant & Uncleanable Ducts: Without code-required access panels, your ductwork cannot be fully inspected or cleaned to "bare metal," making it non-compliant and a high fire risk.
- No Grease Containment = Roof Damage & Environmental Fines: Expelled grease directly impacts your roof, leading to premature breakdown of roofing membranes and potential environmental violations.
- No Makeup Air = Negative Pressure & Operational Issues: A lack of makeup air creates negative pressure, causing doors to slam, external odors to be pulled inside, and your HVAC system to struggle.

### Section: NFPA 96 Standards: Your System Design & Maintenance Mandate (H2)

The National Fire Protection Association (NFPA) 96 Standard is the cornerstone for how commercial kitchen ventilation systems are to be installed, operated, and maintained. Key requirements include:

- Maintaining proper clearances between ducts and combustible materials to prevent fire spread.
- Ensuring liquid-tight welded ducts with the correct slope for safe grease drainage.
- Mandating access doors and hinge kits for complete and safe cleaning access.
- Requiring routine cleaning schedules based on your cooking volume to prevent hazardous grease accumulation.

### Section: Warning Signs: Your Ventilation System Needs Attention (H2)
